Extreme Galaxies

Undergrad Research at University of Arizona

"Extreme" galaxies are galaxies that have formed in environments or through processes thought to be too extreme for typical galaxy formation. This makes them important objects to study to help us expand our current framework of galaxy formation. Using Zooniverse, a popular citizen science platform, I built a project called Blobs and Blurs: Extreme Galaxies in Clusters to search for two types of extreme galaxies: diffuse dwarf galaxies and "blue blobs". Simulations of Merging Galaxy Clusters

NSF-REU at Maria Mitchell Association

Abell 2146 (A2146) is a unique observed cluster merger where the weak lensing and X-ray estimates of the cluster masses are flipped, thus providing an opportunity to better understand, for example, the nature of dark matter and ICM physics during cluster collisions that would lead to this discrepancy. I searched for galaxy cluster mergers analogous to systems like A2146 in the IllustrisTNG cosmological simulations, which account for factors such as complex cluster shape and the surrounding cosmic web (see image below). Planets Embedded in Protoplanetary Disks

NSF-REU at the University of Florida

Detection of disk-embedded planets inside protoplanetary disks remains a critical step in the understanding of planet formation. The gas kinematics of protoplanetary disks hold useful information for this purpose because planets can cause strong and potentially localized perturbations that can indicate their presence without direct observations. Using planet-disk simulataions, I analyzed the kinematics of 12CO gas in synthetic data cubes to see how robustly I could retrieve the location of a planet from simulated perturbations.
